Playing a 12-Bar Blues

Introduction

The 12-bar blues is one of the most important chord progressions in guitar music. It forms the foundation of countless blues, rock, folk, and country songs. If you already know your dominant 7th chords, you’re ready to start playing a 12-bar blues. On this page, you’ll learn three common versions: the standard form, the quick change, and a simple turnaround.

What Is a 12-Bar Blues?

A 12-bar blues is a repeating chord progression made up of 12 measures, or bars. These bars are built using three chords from a key:

  • The I chord (the home chord)
  • The IV chord
  • The V chord

In the key of A, these chords are: A7 (I), D7 (IV), and E7 (V). The pattern stays the same in every key — only the chord names change.

Standard 12-Bar Blues

The standard 12-bar blues is the most basic and widely used version. It stays on the I chord for the first four bars before moving to the IV chord. This gives you time to settle into the groove.

In the key of A, the standard 12-bar blues looks like this:

  • Bars 1–4: A7
  • Bars 5–6: D7
  • Bars 7–8: A7
  • Bar 9: E7
  • Bar 10: D7
  • Bars 11–12: A7

Quick Change 12-Bar Blues

The quick change is a small variation that adds movement early in the progression. Instead of staying on the I chord for four bars, you move to the IV chord in the second bar. This version is very common in blues music.

In the key of A, the quick change version looks like this:

  • Bar 1: A7
  • Bar 2: D7
  • Bars 3–4: A7
  • Bars 5–6: D7
  • Bars 7–8: A7
  • Bar 9: E7
  • Bar 10: D7
  • Bars 11–12: A7

Everything else stays the same — only the second bar changes.

Adding a Turnaround

A turnaround is a short chord movement at the end of the 12-bar blues that leads you smoothly back to the beginning of the progression. It creates tension and makes the return to the I chord feel natural.

One common turnaround replaces the final bar with the V chord:

  • Bar 9: E7
  • Bar 10: D7
  • Bar 11: A7
  • Bar 12: E7

Putting It All Together

Once you’re comfortable with these three versions, you can mix and match them depending on the song. Many blues songs use a quick change, a turnaround, or both. Focus on keeping a steady rhythm and making smooth chord changes.
